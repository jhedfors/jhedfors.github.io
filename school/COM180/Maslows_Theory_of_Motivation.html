Maslow’s Theory of Motivation

Maslow’s theory of motivation is one of the most widely recognized and accepted. He believed that motivation is driven by needs that have not been satisfied. Our needs are ordered in a hierarchy of importance. When a need is mostly satisfied, it no longer drives our behavior and the next higher need becomes the primary motivator.

First, basic needs like food and water must be satisfied and behavior will focus on having these needs fulfilled. Once these physiological needs are satisfied, behavior is motivated by safety needs—such as shelter or employment (a form of security). Next, the need for the sense of belonging, such as from friendship and love, drives behavior. In this stage, communication is used to initiate and maintain relationships. The next level involves fulfilling the need for esteem. Communication is directed towards fulfilling esteem needs by pursuing achievements and maintaining relationships. Finally, high-level needs, like self-fulfillment, are pursued. Behavior and communication concentrate on becoming the best person that one can possibly be.

People’s primary motivation continually moves up and down the hierarchy as their circumstances change.
