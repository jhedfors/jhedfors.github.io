Sensation and Perception

Sensation and perception are two of the most basic psychological functions. Our senses give us information that allows our brain to interpret the world around us. Our actions and high-level thinking start with sensations and perceptions.

Clearly, our sensations and perceptions help us to interpret the world around us. Our perceptions also dictate how we interpret our communications—both the way that we communicate and the way we receive communications from others. On any given day, we’re inundated by information. We start the perception process by selecting information, and we make sense of it by organizing it and interpreting it. These actions often happen in seconds. Once we have an interpretation, we then have to go back to our own knowledge bank and negotiate how the new information will fit into our already established truths.

To increase your own interpersonal effectiveness, it is important for you to understand the process you go through as you perceive verbal and nonverbal communication from others, the external and internal factors that influence your perception, and the ways that you might typically respond or react to various types of communication.

The perception process involves four steps:

  • Selection
  • Organization
  • Interpretation
  • Negotiation
